Paty's Open to the Public After Remodeling

Gino Renzulli　　07/12/2024

The boy I nanny looks at me with the slightest mischief. The twinkle in his eye tells me a joke is coming. He says, “knock knock.” Entertaining what I assume to be one of his routine jokes, I ask who is there. He replies, stone cold and with zero reservation, “It’s your dad. He’s been gone for 35 years and has finally returned with the milk.” He holds an imaginary microphone to my mouth, as if interviewing me, and states, “How does that make you feel.” Yet, his ending remark is not a question, as he holds no care for how I actually feel, but rather an ending statement that perfectly annotates the complexity of his joke. The past four years of not having my dad with me pass behind eyes like rolling film. He has no idea how he’s caught me, how good his joke actually is. It hurts to be burned by a nine year old.

In the past three months I have spent lengths of time with three different groups of children. All ranging from nine to fourteen years old. All living in different areas, of varying racial and economic backgrounds. Some with older siblings, some without. One family with an actively married couple, one divorced with shared custody and one widowed. Multi-gendered, racially diverse from one another, all of varying economical standings and from differing neighborhoods; two from completely different states. In each of these groups I have observed commonality within the children depicting ruthless behavior and emotionless positions. Frequently dealt out are jokes meant to sting, comments meant to chip away at another person’s self esteem. Unfortunately, no one is safe and everyone is left reeling from blows to their ego.
